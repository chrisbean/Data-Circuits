GROUP BY and HAVING

Querying discrete clusters of information is very useful for SQL users.

For example:

Let's say you work at a major motion picture company. You're looking to make the next big summer blockbuster, but are trying to find the perfect lead. What can you do to easily comb through historical data?

Using an AGGREGATE function might only return an outlier. In order to get a more historically informed pull of information, we can use GROUP BY function.

Syntax

SELECT column_name, aggregate_function(column_name)
FROM table_name
WHERE column_name operator value
GROUP BY column_name;
SELECT title , studio , year
FROM film
WHERE revM < 500
GROUP BY studio;

GROUPBY allows you to pull particular parts of data, and quickly capture an idea. This is much easier than exporting a large part of the database to spreadsheet software and using a sort function

So it turns out you're not as interested in leads that are movies from more than 5 years ago. What if you wanted to only look for leads who starred in movies in the past 5 years

HAVING adds another level of filtering to your grouped queries, beyond the capabilities of WHERE

HAVING adds another level of filtering to your grouped queries, beyond the capabilities of WHERE

SELECT column_name, aggregate_function(column_name)
FROM table_name
WHERE column_name operator value
GROUP BY column_name;
HAVING column_name operator value

SELECT title , studio
FROM film
WHERE rev < 500
GROUP BY studio
HAVING year >= 2010;
The query return would look like this:
title studio year
How to Train Your Dragon P/DW 2010
Django Unchained Wein. 2012

HAVING allows you to add another level of granularity to your queries. Since it works with the operators and aggregate functions, it affords you the ability to have extremely precise data pulls.
