The Analytical Process

Your dive into data analysis should be guided
by a question you want answered.


  • What are the health effects of drinking coffee?
  • Who will be this year’s World Cup champions?
  • How does my government spend its money?
  • How will graduating next year affect my future earnings?

Figuring out how to use data to answer these questions is not easy.

Data analysis is a scientific process.

One film review might provide enough data for you to decide how to spend your Saturday night.

But what if your screenwriter friend asks for your help. She's writing a script and needs to know, "what genre of film performs best at the box office?"


First, make sure you understand the problem or question you’d like answered.

Your friend wants to know:
“What genre of film performs best at the box office?”



  • The performance of the film, what you’d like to explain, is called the dependent variable.

First, make sure you understand the problem or question you’d like answered.

Your friend wants to know:
“What genre of film performs best at the box office?”


  • The genre of the film, what you think may cause or predict the dependent variable, is called an independent variable.

Next, you need to identify what data you need and compile it in a spreadsheet.

We'll use a site called Box Office Mojo, which tracks box office and movie publication data.

Here we’ll find a list of every Hollywood film released over the last 50 years and data on their title, studio, gross revenue domestic and foreign, year released, MPAA rating, and ticket sales.

  • All the pieces of information that you gather is called a dataset.

It’s crucial that you understand what’s in your dataset.

Data comes in all sorts of shapes and sizes.

The number of tickets sold and the total revenue grossed by our films are examples of quantitative data.

The genre and the titles of our films are examples of qualitative data.

  • Is the data quantitative—that is, information that can be measured and expressed with numbers?
  • Is the data qualitative—that is, information that can't actually be measured?

Before we analyze it, we need to make sure our data is “clean.”

Maybe someone at IMDB wrote the wrong release date for Gone with the Wind, and we would need to correct that.

Additionally, to compare the revenue of films across 50 years we need to adjust for inflation. So historical dollar amounts should be converted to 2015 USD.


  • Raw data is data that has not yet been manipulated or processed. It usually contains errors, duplicates and missing data.

Finally, we get to the good stuff!

A smart strategy is to start by looking at one variable—let’s say genre.

What is the most common genre in the data set? How often is each genre in the top 10 highest-grossing films each year?

Making graphs with our data makes it easier for us to spot patterns and gain new insights.

  • This approach to data analysis is known as Exploratory Analysis.

Your presentation needs to tell your story.

Our data problem began with a question, and through analysis, it should end with a clear narrative.

As the great Edward Tufte says, “There are right ways and wrong ways to show data; there are displays that reveal the truth and displays that do not.”


  • Data Visualizations do not simply display information, they tell a story to your audience.

This six-step process will ensure
you draw actionable insights from your data.




But it shouldn't end there!

The data you collect and and the analysis you generate can be used to address new questions.
